What You'll Do

Great stories don't just tell us what happened‚Äîthey show us something true about life. In this assessment, you'll dig into a short story of your choice and uncover the theme the author wants readers to understand. Then you'll prove it with evidence from the text.

The Big Picture

You will write a ~500-word analytical essay that identifies a theme in your chosen short story and supports your interpretation with specific evidence from the text. This is about showing how authors communicate meaning through their craft.

üìù Criterion B: Organizing

  • Communication: Write for a specific purpose and audience; structure information logically; use appropriate register and academic vocabulary

Your Task

1 Choose Your Story

Select one story from the five options provided. Consider your interests‚Äîdo you prefer realistic drama, magical realism, psychological mystery, adventure, or dystopian sci-fi? Check the Story Options tab to read the hooks and make your choice.

2 Read & Identify Theme

Read your story carefully, annotating as you go. Identify a theme‚Äîa central message or insight about life that the author communicates. Remember: theme is not a single word (like "love") but a statement about that topic (like "True love requires sacrifice").

3 Gather Evidence

Find 2-3 specific pieces of evidence from the text that support your theme. Look for how the author uses characterisation, symbolism, setting, conflict, or other literary techniques to develop this theme.

4 Write Your Essay

Write a ~500-word essay that presents your theme statement and proves it with evidence. Your essay should have:

  • Introduction: Hook, context, and your theme statement
  • Body Paragraphs: Evidence + analysis showing how the author develops theme
  • Conclusion: Reinforce your theme and its significance

üìã Requirements Checklist

  • Word count: Approximately 500 words (450-550 acceptable)
  • Format: Google Doc, 12pt font, double-spaced
  • Title: Include your name, the story title, and author
  • Evidence: At least 2-3 direct quotes from the text with page/paragraph references
  • Terminology: Use literary terms accurately (theme, symbolism, characterisation, imagery, etc.)
  • Structure: Clear introduction with theme statement, analytical body paragraphs, and conclusion
  • Style: Academic register with varied sentence structures
  • Accuracy: Proofread for grammar, punctuation, and spelling

üí° Tips for Success

  • Criterion A: Your theme should be arguable‚Äînot a summary. Explain HOW quotes support your theme.
  • Criterion B: Each body paragraph needs a clear topic sentence. Use transitions between ideas.
  • Criterion C: Show your own thinking‚Äîdon't just summarise. What new insight did you gain?
  • Criterion D: Use present tense ("The author shows..."). Vary your sentence openings. Proofread!

Tips for Parents

This summative assessment asks students to independently analyse a short story and write an essay identifying its theme. Here's how you can support your student effectively.

‚úÖ Helpful Ways to Support

  • Ask: "What do you think the author wants readers to understand about life?"
  • Ask: "How does that quote connect to your theme?"
  • Help them talk through their ideas before writing
  • Encourage them to read the story more than once
  • Provide a quiet workspace and help manage their time
  • Celebrate their effort and thinking, not just the grade

üö´ Please Avoid

  • Telling them what the theme is
  • Writing or heavily editing their essay
  • Googling "theme of [story title]" and sharing results
  • Choosing quotes for them
  • Rewriting their sentences to "make them better"

üí¨ Good Questions to Ask

  • "What changed for the main character by the end of the story?"
  • "What lesson could a reader learn from this story?"
  • "Why do you think the author included that detail?"
  • "Can you explain your theme to me in your own words?"
  • "What evidence from the story proves your point?"

The Big Picture

What is this assignment?

You're going to write an original short story ‚Äî about 1,500 words ‚Äî that demonstrates your command of the craft of fiction. Strong short stories aren't just plots; they use carefully chosen language, deliberate structure, and layered meaning to say something worth saying.

This assignment has two stages: first, a Story Planner where you make and justify your creative decisions, and then the story itself. The planner isn't busywork ‚Äî it's where your best ideas will come from.

At a Glance

What you'll do

Stage 1 ‚Äî Story Planner (Formative ¬∑ Criterion A)

Before you write a word of your story, you'll complete a written planner that documents the creative decisions behind it. You'll identify and justify your conflict, setting, protagonist, and point of view. This is submitted separately and you'll receive feedback before you draft.

Stage 2 ‚Äî The Short Story (Summative ¬∑ Criteria B & C)

Stage 1

The Story Planner

For each of the four sections below, you need to make a decision and then explain your reasoning. Your planner should be written in full sentences and demonstrate genuine analytical thinking ‚Äî not just describe what you'll do, but explain why.

1 of 4

Conflict

Identify the type of conflict your story will use. Consider: character vs. character, character vs. self, character vs. society, character vs. nature, character vs. fate/supernatural.

Explain why this type of conflict is the right choice for the story you want to tell. How does it shape what happens? What does it allow you to explore thematically?

Why is this conflict the engine of your story, and not a different type?

2 of 4

Setting

Describe the time and place of your story. Then explain how your setting connects to both your protagonist and your theme ‚Äî a setting that's merely a backdrop is a missed opportunity.

How does where and when your story happens shape who your character is and what the story is ultimately about?

3 of 4

Protagonist

Describe your protagonist. Who are they? What do they want, and what do they fear? What makes them interesting as a character?

Then explain why this particular character is the right choice for your story. Could a different kind of protagonist have told this story better ‚Äî and why not?

Why is this person the right character to carry your story's weight?

4 of 4

Point of View

Choose your narrative point of view: first person, third person limited, or third person omniscient.

You must justify your choice. Think about what each POV allows you to do ‚Äî and what it prevents. Why is your chosen POV the best lens for this particular story?

What does your chosen POV allow you to do that the others would not?

Criterion A ¬∑ Analyzing (Formative)
Your planner will be assessed on the quality of your analytical thinking ‚Äî specifically, how well you explain and justify your creative decisions using literary understanding. Strong planners make clear connections between craft choices and the effects they create.

Stage 2

Writing Your Story

Your story must be approximately 1,500 words. Below are the requirements ‚Äî but remember: the best stories use these elements because they serve the story, not because they tick a box.

Characterization

Bringing Characters to Life

Your story must use both types of characterization:

Direct Characterization

The narrator or another character explicitly tells us something about a character's personality, appearance, or nature.

Indirect Characterization

We infer what a character is like through their actions, dialogue, thoughts, appearance, or how others react to them ‚Äî without being told directly.

Theme

Saying Something Without Saying It

Implied Theme

Your story must have a theme ‚Äî a meaningful idea about human experience ‚Äî that emerges through what happens and how, not through a character stating it outright. The reader should feel the theme without you spelling it out.

Plot Structure

The Shape of Your Story

Your story must use all six parts of plot structure ‚Äî in whatever proportions serve your narrative best.

1

Exposition

Establish your world ‚Äî setting, characters, and situation ‚Äî giving readers what they need to enter the story.

2

Inciting Incident

The moment that disrupts the status quo and sets the central conflict in motion.

3

Rising Action

A series of events that build tension as your protagonist works toward ‚Äî or struggles against ‚Äî the conflict.

4

Climax

The turning point ‚Äî the moment of highest tension where something decisive happens or is decided.

5

Falling Action

The aftermath of the climax ‚Äî events that begin to resolve the tension.

6

Resolution

The new equilibrium ‚Äî how things end up, and what has changed.

Assessment

How Your Story Will Be Graded

Criterion B ¬∑ Organizing
How effectively you organize and structure your story. This includes the coherence of your plot, use of paragraphing and sequencing, and how well the structure serves the narrative's meaning.
Criterion C ¬∑ Producing Text
How effectively you use language to create effect. This includes your use of literary devices, the quality and precision of your word choices, how well your style serves your story's purpose, and the overall control of your writing.
For Families

How to Support Your Writer

This assignment asks students to make genuine creative decisions and write an original short story. Your role as a parent is not to shape the story ‚Äî it's to support the conditions in which your student can do their best thinking.

üóìÔ∏è Understanding the Timeline

  • The assignment has two stages: the Story Planner (submitted first) and the Short Story (submitted after planner feedback).
  • Encourage your student not to leave either stage to the last moment ‚Äî the planner genuinely improves the story.
  • Ask your student what the deadlines are and help them block out time to work in advance.

üí¨ Questions That Help (Without Doing the Work)

  • "What's the most interesting thing about your main character?"
  • "If someone read your story without any explanation, what do you think they'd walk away thinking or feeling?"
  • "Why did you choose to tell it from that point of view? What would change if it were different?"
  • "Where in the story do you feel the tension most? How did you build up to that moment?"
  • "Is there a moment in the story where you show something rather than just tell it?"

‚úÖ Helpful Ways to Support

  • Read through the assignment requirements with your student so you both understand what's expected.
  • Ask your student to explain their story idea out loud ‚Äî talking through a story often helps clarify it.
  • Encourage a first draft that's imperfect ‚Äî the goal is to get ideas down, then revise.
  • Help create a distraction-free environment during drafting sessions.

‚ö†Ô∏è What to Avoid

  • Don't write or rewrite any part of the story ‚Äî even with good intentions, this takes away from your student's learning and their grade reflects their work.
  • Don't dictate plot ideas or character decisions ‚Äî ask questions instead and let your student arrive at answers independently.
  • Don't use AI tools to generate the story ‚Äî students should discuss any use of AI tools with their teacher first.
  • Don't stress perfection ‚Äî this assignment is designed to stretch students, and some struggle is part of the learning.

STATION TWO

The Unreliable Narrator

Re-Reading for Misdirection: What Did He Choose Not to Tell Us?

You know the twist: The narrator of "A Study in Emerald" is not Watson—he is Colonel Sebastian Moran. His "friend" is not Sherlock Holmes—he is Professor James Moriarty. The "villains" they hunt? The real Holmes and Watson, who are trying to overthrow the alien rulers.

Now that you know, look back at these excerpts. What clues were hidden in plain sight?

Your Task

  1. Read each excerpt knowing what you now know about the narrator's identity
  2. Identify what the narrator omits, obscures, or misdirects us about
  3. Consider: Is he lying? Or just... not telling the whole truth?
  4. Record your findings on your worksheet

Excerpts for Re-Examination

1

"That I survived was a miracle, but survive I did, and I returned to England with my nerves in shreds and tatters. The place that leech-like mouth had touched me was tattooed forever, frog-white, into the skin of my now-withered shoulder. I had once been a crack-shot. Now I had nothing, save a fear of the world-beneath-the-world akin to panic..."

— Section 1: The New Friend

Now that you know: Watson's wound was from a Jezail bullet in Afghanistan. This narrator's wound came from a "leech-like mouth." What does this detail reveal about the world of this story—and why might a first-time reader overlook it?
2

"She was called Victoria, because she had beaten us in battle, seven hundred years before, and she was called Gloriana, because she was glorious, and she was called the Queen, because the human mouth was not shaped to say her true name. She was huge, huger than I had imagined possible, and she squatted in the shadows staring down at us, without moving."

— Section 3: The Palace

Now that you know: The narrator describes meeting Queen Victoria as if this is an honor. He doesn't question why she's inhuman—he accepts it. What does his acceptance tell us about him and the world he serves?
3

"'I must warn you not to be such a fathead,' said my friend. 'Because it was the Restorationists that killed Prince Franz Drago. They murder, they kill, in a vain effort to force our masters to leave us alone in the darkness. The Prince was killed by a rache—it's an old term for a hunting dog, Inspector, as you would know if you had looked in a dictionary.'"

— Section 5: The Skin and the Pit

Now that you know: Notice the phrase "our masters." The narrator's friend openly refers to the alien rulers as masters—and frames those fighting for human freedom as murderers. How does this passage read differently when you understand which side the narrator is actually on?
4

"'This is a friend of mine, Mister Sebastian. He is not of the profession.'"

— Section 4: The Performance (introducing the narrator to the actor Sherry Vernet)

Now that you know: The narrator's friend casually reveals the narrator's first name: Sebastian. Combined with the final signature "S_____ M_____ Major (Ret'd)," what famous Doyle villain does this point to?
5

"I kept the note. I have said things in this retelling of events that are not to be said. If I were a sensible man I would burn all these pages, but then, as my friend taught me, even ashes can give up their secrets. Instead, I shall place these papers in a strongbox at my bank with instructions that the box may not be opened until long after anyone now living is dead."

— Final section

Now that you know: The narrator admits he has "said things... not to be said" and hides this document. What secrets has he revealed—perhaps accidentally? What might he still be hiding even now?

THE FINAL CLUES — HIDDEN NAMES

The narrator meets an actor named Sherry Vernet backstage at the theatre.

Later, his friend reveals: "So that Sherry Vernet fellow..." / "Is a Restorationist. Exactly."

The story ends with the narrator's signature:

S_____ M_____
Major (Ret'd)
Baker Street, London, New Albion, 1881.
The Hidden Names: "Vernet" is the maiden name of Sherlock Holmes's grandmother (mentioned in Doyle's "The Greek Interpreter"). The actor Sherry Vernet is Holmes in disguise. The narrator signs only his initials—"S. M."—which, combined with "Mister Sebastian" and "Major," points to Colonel Sebastian Moran, Moriarty's chief lieutenant in Doyle's stories.
